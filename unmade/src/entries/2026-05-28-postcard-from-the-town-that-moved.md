The postcard showed a main street that no longer matched the ground. The church had been moved uphill, the grocery became storage, and the river kept the old coordinates for itself.

It was never mailed because the sender could not decide whether the destination was a person, a place, or a version of the map that had stopped being true.

## Reverse Side

The message on the back got as far as the weather. After that, the sender kept starting over. The old town name felt sentimental. The new one felt like an administrative correction. Neither matched the photograph, which had become a polite lie by the time the card was bought.

There is a crease through the stamp corner, probably from being carried too long in a wallet. That small damage matters more than the view. It proves the card almost entered the world, then stayed close enough to become private.

The postcard is not asking where to go. It is asking which version of a place deserves the address.
