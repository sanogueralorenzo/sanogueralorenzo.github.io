The object was supposed to be small enough to keep in a coat pocket and precise enough to make a ceiling feel like a field. A thumbwheel would set the month. A second ring would choose the latitude. The smallest motion would scatter an old sky across whatever room had become too bright to see the real one.

The prototype never progressed beyond cardboard, but the cardboard was handled like a sacred instrument. Each puncture was labeled. Each constellation was drawn twice: once correctly, once the way it was remembered from childhood.

It stayed on paper because the best part was not the mechanism. It was the idea that wonder could be made portable without being made smaller.

## Lens Dust

The planetarium is filed as an invention, but it behaves more like a repair. It answers a civic wound: skies erased by light, childhood orientation replaced by weather apps, the universe made available as data but unavailable as a ceiling.

The hard line to draw is between repair and performance: at what point does giving the stars back become a show about loss instead of a working sky?
