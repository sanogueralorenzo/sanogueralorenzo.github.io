The guidebook began with useful warnings: which doors locked behind you, which tunnels carried sound, which shops kept unofficial hours for people who worked before morning. It described service corridors, basement economies, stairwells with informal names, and the shortcuts used by people whose work happened while the visible city slept.

The tone changed halfway through chapter four. Sentences became evasive. Directions became ethics. The writer began replacing turns with refusals: do not enter if you are only curious; do not photograph the door; do not mistake access for permission.

It stopped when description began to feel like betrayal. Some maps are completed by refusing to publish the route.

## Sublevel Errata

The guidebook is unbuilt because its accuracy became dangerous. To describe the city under the city was to make it available to people who had not earned its trust.

What still bothers the record is this: whether an archive can honor hidden infrastructure without becoming another instrument of extraction.
